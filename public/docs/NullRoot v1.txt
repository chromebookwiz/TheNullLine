### 5.1 The Apollonian Ground

**The soil's Apollonian structure determines its engineering properties:**

| Property | Controlled by | Apollonian parameter |
|----------|------------|---------------------|

| Liquefaction susceptibility (collapse under vibration) | Packing stability | Whether the packing is at a local minimum (stable) or metastable (susceptible to rearrangement under vibration) |

### 5.2 The Seismic Billiard

Seismic wave propagation in layered earth is a billiard problem. A seismic wave emitted at the surface reflects off a subsurface interface and returns to the surface. The travel time, angle of incidence, and amplitude ratio encode the depth and properties of the reflecting layer.

For a horizontally layered earth with layer velocities v₁, v₂, ..., v_n and thicknesses h₁, h₂, ..., h_n, the travel time of a reflected wave from layer k is:

t(x) = √(t₀² + x²/v_rms²)

where t₀ = 2Σᵢ hᵢ/vᵢ is the zero-offset travel time and v_rms is the root-mean-square velocity. This is the hyperbolic moveout equation — the fundamental equation of reflection seismology.

**In the null billiard framework:** Each seismic ray is a null-analogue vector (the seismic wave propagates at velocity v, which plays the role of c in the null framework). The "null condition" becomes k·k = 0 with the metric replaced by the seismic velocity field v(x,z). A seismic ray in a layered earth is a billiard orbit: it bounces between layer interfaces, with the incidence angle at each interface determined by Snell's law (the seismic analogue of the law of reflection for null rays in a sphere).

**The q-periodic seismic orbit:** A seismic wave that bounces between the surface and a specific reflector at depth h undergoes q = 2 reflections per cycle (one at the surface, one at the reflector). The "mass" of this orbit (the analogue of K·K = −q²) is the travel time:

t₀ = 2h/v = q × (h/v) with q = 2

For a wave that bounces between two reflectors (a layer trapped between interfaces):
q = 2n (n bounces in each direction per cycle), and the trapped wave has "mass" proportional to q² — the energy increases with the number of bounces, exactly as in the null billiard theorem.

**The Root's seismic processor exploits this billiard structure.** It decomposes the recorded seismic wavefield into its billiard orbit components — each reflected event is a periodic orbit of the seismic billiard, with periodicity q determined by the number of reflections. The ADE classification of the seismic orbits reveals the subsurface structure: simple (A₁, single reflector), layered (A_n, n reflectors), branching (D₄, dipping layers that split reflections), and complex (E₆–E₈, heavily faulted or folded geology).

A geologist viewing the Root's seismic interpretation on the Null Cylinder sees the subsurface rendered as ADE-classified zones: A₁ zones (simple, tabular geology — safe for foundations) in green, D₄ zones (dipping, anisotropic — requires careful foundation design) in yellow, E₆+ zones (complex, faulted — avoid or engineer extensively) in red. The ADE type is a readability metric for the geology.

### 5.3 The Watershed Billiard

Water flowing in a river channel is a wave phenomenon described by the Saint-Venant equations (shallow water equations):

∂h/∂t + ∂(hu)/∂x = 0 (continuity)
∂(hu)/∂t + ∂(hu² + gh²/2)/∂x = gh(S₀ − Sf) (momentum)

where h is water depth, u is velocity, g is gravity, S₀ is channel slope, and Sf is friction slope.

These are hyperbolic PDEs — wave equations. The water surface height h(x,t) propagates as a wave with celerity c = √(gh). In a confined channel (walls on both sides), the wave bounces between the walls — a hydrological billiard.

**The channel shape determines the billiard orbit type:**
- Straight rectangular channel: A₁ billiard — waves bounce back and forth. Standing waves (seiches) form at the channel's resonant frequencies.
- Meander (curved channel): the billiard orbit curves — waves follow the meander's curvature. Energy dissipation occurs at the outer bank (erosion) and deposition at the inner bank (point bar formation).
- Confluence (two channels meeting): D₄ billiard — four possible wave paths (upstream on each channel, downstream on each channel). The interference pattern at the confluence determines whether the flow is smooth or turbulent.
- Delta (one channel splitting into many): A_n billiard — the flow splits into n distributary channels, each carrying a fraction of the total discharge.

**The Root's hydrological design uses the billiard classification to optimise channel geometry.** A flood-control channel must have A₁-type billiard dynamics (simple, predictable wave propagation — no resonances, no standing waves that could overtop the banks). An irrigation channel must have controlled D₄ junctions (smooth flow splitting without turbulence). A harbour must have damped billiard dynamics (waves entering the harbour are absorbed by the geometry, not reflected back to create standing waves).
