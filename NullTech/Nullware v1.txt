## I. MATERIAL SCIENCE: CHOOSING THE RIGHT SPHERE

The sphere material determines Q-factor, nonlinear response, thermal stability, and fabrication method. Different computational tasks demand different material trade-offs.

### I.1 Material Candidates

**Fused Silica (SiO₂)**
- Q-factor: up to 10⁹ (record: 8 × 10⁹, Grudinin et al. 2006)
- Refractive index: n = 1.444 @ 1550 nm
- Nonlinear coefficient: n₂ = 2.2 × 10⁻²⁰ m²/W (weak — needs high Q to compensate)
- Thermal coefficient: dn/dT = +1.1 × 10⁻⁵ /K (moderate drift)
- Fabrication: Melt optical fibre tip with CO₂ laser. Sphere forms by surface tension. Diameter controlled by fibre diameter and melt time. Reproducible to ±2 μm.
- Best for: Long-lifetime storage orbits, high-q periodic modes (q up to ~10⁹), passive memory cells. The workhorse material for Phase 0–2.

**Calcium Fluoride (CaF₂)**
- Q-factor: up to 10¹¹ (record holder for crystalline resonators)
- Refractive index: n = 1.426 @ 1550 nm
- Nonlinear coefficient: n₂ = 1.9 × 10⁻²⁰ m²/W (comparable to silica)
- Thermal coefficient: dn/dT = −1.1 × 10⁻⁵ /K (negative — enables thermal self-locking)
- Fabrication: Diamond turning and hand polishing of CaF₂ blanks. Produces mm-scale discs rather than spheres. More expensive, but the extreme Q extends photon lifetime to ~10 ms.
- Best for: Precision frequency references, ultra-long computation windows, and any task where the billiard orbit must complete more than ~10⁹ reflections (high-q orbits with q > 10⁹).

**Lithium Niobate (LiNbO₃)**
- Q-factor: up to 10⁸ (limited by absorption)
- Refractive index: n = 2.21 (ordinary) @ 1550 nm
- Nonlinear coefficient: d₃₃ = 25.2 pm/V (extremely strong second-order nonlinearity)
- Electro-optic coefficient: r₃₃ = 30.8 pm/V
- Fabrication: Thin-film LiNbO₃ on insulator (LNOI) microring resonators, etched photolithographically. Not true spheres — microdiscs or microrings — but support equivalent WGM physics.
- Best for: CAPTURE gate operations. The strong χ² nonlinearity enables efficient photon-photon interaction at microwatt power. Electro-optic tunability allows real-time angle control (programming the orbit electronically). This is the active computation material.

**Silicon Nitride (Si₃N₄)**
- Q-factor: up to 3 × 10⁷ (improving rapidly with Damascene process)
- Refractive index: n = 1.99 @ 1550 nm
- Nonlinear coefficient: n₂ = 2.4 × 10⁻¹⁹ m²/W (10× silica — strong Kerr effect)
- Fabrication: CMOS-foundry compatible. Photolithographic waveguides and microring resonators. Mass-producible.
- Best for: Scalable multi-sphere arrays (Phase 3–4). The CMOS compatibility means existing semiconductor fabs can manufacture NSC chips. Lower Q is offset by stronger nonlinearity and massive parallelism.

**Barium Titanate (BaTiO₃)**
- Q-factor: ~10⁶ (limited by scattering in polycrystalline films)
- Refractive index: n = 2.36 @ 1550 nm
- Nonlinear coefficient: Pockels coefficient r₄₂ = 820 pm/V (among the highest known)
- Best for: Ultra-fast switching of the CAPTURE gate. The enormous electro-optic response allows sub-nanosecond gate control, enabling clock speeds above 1 GHz for the NSC's active operations.

### I.2 Hybrid Material Strategy

No single material optimises all three operations simultaneously. The engineering solution is a hybrid architecture:

- **Memory spheres** (REFLECT + store): Fused silica or CaF₂. Maximum Q, maximum photon lifetime, maximum orbit fidelity.
- **Gate spheres** (CAPTURE): LiNbO₃ microdiscs or BaTiO₃-coated silica. Strong nonlinearity for efficient photon-photon interaction.
- **Bus waveguides** (inter-sphere coupling): Si₃N₄ on-chip waveguides. CMOS-compatible, low loss, foundry-fabricable.
- **Detector**: InGaAs single-photon avalanche diode (SPAD) for 1550 nm. Dark count < 1 kHz, timing jitter < 50 ps. Commercial off-the-shelf.

This mirrors electronic computing's separation of SRAM (storage), logic gates (computation), and metal interconnects (routing) — but entirely in photonics.

## II. SPHERE GEOMETRY AND ORBIT FAMILIES

### II.1 Sphere Size and Mode Structure

The WGM mode spectrum of a sphere of radius R and refractive index n is characterised by three quantum numbers:

- **l** (angular momentum): Number of wavelengths around the equator. Maps to billiard orbit periodicity q.
- **m** (azimuthal): Tilt of the orbit plane. |m| ≤ l. m = l is equatorial; m < l tilts toward the poles.
- **n_r** (radial): Number of intensity maxima in the radial direction. n_r = 1 is the fundamental (highest Q); higher n_r modes penetrate deeper into the sphere.

The resonant wavelengths are approximately:

λ_{l,n_r} ≈ 2πnR / (l + ½ + α_{n_r}(l + ½)^{1/3} + ...)

where α_{n_r} are Airy function zeros. The free spectral range (FSR) — the wavelength spacing between consecutive l modes — is:

FSR ≈ λ² / (2πnR)

**Design table for different sphere radii:**

| Radius R | Circumference | Round-trip time | FSR @ 1550 nm | Max l (@ 1550 nm) | Computation window (Q=10⁸) |
|----------|--------------|-----------------|---------------|-------------------|---------------------------|
| 25 μm | 157 μm | 0.76 ps | 10.8 nm | ~147 | ~76 ns |
| 50 μm | 314 μm | 1.51 ps | 5.4 nm | ~293 | ~151 ns |
| 100 μm | 628 μm | 3.03 ps | 2.7 nm | ~586 | ~303 ns |
| 250 μm | 1571 μm | 7.57 ps | 1.08 nm | ~1465 | ~757 ns |
| 500 μm | 3142 μm | 15.1 ps | 0.54 nm | ~2930 | ~1.51 μs |

**Interpretation:** A 100 μm sphere supports ~586 distinct angular momentum modes (l values) at 1550 nm, each corresponding to a different periodic orbit. The computation window at Q = 10⁸ is ~303 ns — enough for ~10⁸ reflections. Smaller spheres have wider FSR (easier to resolve modes spectrally) but fewer modes; larger spheres have more modes but tighter FSR (requiring higher spectral resolution to read out).

### II.2 Orbit Families by Geometry

**Equatorial orbits (m = l):** The photon circulates in the equatorial plane. These are the 2D billiard orbits of Theorem 2.1. A q-periodic equatorial orbit traces a regular q-gon inscribed in the equator. These are the simplest to excite (standard tapered-fibre coupling) and detect (output from the same equatorial plane).

- q = 3 (triangle): Incidence angle α = π/3 = 60°. Three reflections. K·K = −9. Emergent mass m = 3.
- q = 4 (square): α = π/4 = 45°. Four reflections. K·K = −16. Mass m = 4.
- q = 5 (pentagon): α = π/5 = 36°. Five reflections. K·K = −25. Mass m = 5.
- q = 6 (hexagon): α = π/6 = 30°. Six reflections. K·K = −36. Mass m = 6.

**Polar orbits (m = 0):** The photon passes through the poles. These orbits span a great circle perpendicular to the equator. They are harder to excite with standard equatorial couplers but provide an independent set of modes for parallel computation.

**Tilted orbits (0 < |m| < l):** The photon traces a path on a cone of half-angle θ = arccos(m/l). These provide intermediate modes between equatorial and polar. A sphere simultaneously supporting equatorial and tilted orbits of different (l, m) performs parallel computations in independent mode families — the photonic analogue of SIMD (single instruction, multiple data).

**Star orbits (p > 1):** When α = pπ/q with p > 1, the orbit traces a star polygon {q/p}. For example, α = 2π/5 gives the pentagram {5/2}: the photon hits every other vertex of a pentagon. These orbits have the same periodicity q but different winding numbers p. They occupy the same l mode but with different internal phase structure, providing additional addressable states within each mode.

| Orbit type | Symbol | α | q bounces | Winding p | Null mass m | WGM mode mapping |
|-----------|--------|---|-----------|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Triangle | {3/1} | π/3 | 3 | 1 | 3 | l ≡ 0 mod 3 |
| Square | {4/1} | π/4 | 4 | 1 | 4 | l ≡ 0 mod 4 |
| Pentagon | {5/1} | π/5 | 5 | 1 | 5 | l ≡ 0 mod 5 |
| Pentagram | {5/2} | 2π/5 | 5 | 2 | 5 | l ≡ 0 mod 5, 2nd harmonic |
| Hexagon | {6/1} | π/6 | 6 | 1 | 6 | l ≡ 0 mod 6 |
| Star of David | {6/2} | 2π/6 | 6 | 2 | 6 | Degenerate: two triangles |
| Heptagon | {7/1} | π/7 | 7 | 1 | 7 | l ≡ 0 mod 7 |
| Heptagram | {7/2} | 2π/7 | 7 | 2 | 7 | l ≡ 0 mod 7, 2nd harmonic |
| Heptagram | {7/3} | 3π/7 | 7 | 3 | 7 | l ≡ 0 mod 7, 3rd harmonic |

### II.3 Three-Dimensional Orbits: Platonic and Archimedean

In a true sphere (not a disc), photons can trace three-dimensional periodic orbits corresponding to the vertices of polyhedra inscribed in the sphere. These are not WGM modes in the traditional sense — they require excitation of multiple (l, m) modes simultaneously — but they correspond to the ADE exceptional types.

**Tetrahedral orbit (E₆-related):** 4 vertices, 6 edges, 4 faces. Requires simultaneous excitation of modes with l = 3, m = ±3, ±1, 0. The orbit visits 4 reflection points arranged as a regular tetrahedron. This is the 3D analogue of the triangle orbit, with the tetrahedral symmetry group 2T (order 24) classified by E₆ in the McKay correspondence.

**Octahedral orbit (E₇-related):** 6 vertices, 12 edges, 8 faces. Requires modes with l = 4 and specific m combinations. Octahedral symmetry group 2O (order 48) maps to E₇.

**Icosahedral orbit (E₈-related):** 12 vertices, 30 edges, 20 faces. Requires modes with l = 6 and specific m combinations. Icosahedral symmetry group 2I (order 120) maps to E₈. This is the maximum-symmetry 3D orbit and provides the densest information encoding.

**Excitation strategy for 3D orbits:** Use multiple input fibres at different latitudes and longitudes on the sphere, each injecting coherent photons at controlled phases. The relative phases of the inputs determine the 3D orbital geometry. A tetrahedral orbit needs 4 input fibres; an icosahedral orbit needs at least 6 (one per axis of symmetry). Alternatively, use a spatial light modulator (SLM) to shape the input beam into the desired 3D mode pattern before coupling to the sphere.

## III. TASK-SPECIFIC CONFIGURATIONS

### III.1 Integer Arithmetic Unit

**Task:** Addition, subtraction, multiplication of integers up to ~500.

**Configuration:** Single large sphere (R = 250 μm, Q > 10⁸) in fused silica. One input fibre with electro-optic phase modulator. One output fibre with scanning Fabry-Pérot analyser.

**Operations:**

*Addition (a + b):* Inject photon at mode l = a, then photon at mode l = b. Nonlinear four-wave mixing generates mode l = a + b. Read out dominant output mode.

*Multiplication (a × b):* Use cascaded frequency conversion. Inject mode l = a. Apply a nonlinear phase modulation at frequency proportional to b (using an external RF drive on an electro-optic coating). The modulation generates sidebands at l = a × b via the photorefractive effect. Alternatively, use b successive CAPTURE operations of a-orbits.

*Subtraction (a − b, a > b):* Inject mode l = a with one polarisation and mode l = b with orthogonal polarisation. Difference-frequency generation produces mode l = a − b.

**Advantages over electronic arithmetic:** Parallel multi-operand addition is natural — inject N photons at modes l₁, ..., l_N simultaneously, and four-wave mixing produces all pairwise sums in a single computation cycle (~ns timescale). An electronic adder tree for N operands requires O(N log N) gate delays; the sphere performs it in O(1) optical cycles.

**Estimated performance:** Addition in ~3 ns (one round-trip time at R = 250 μm, Q = 10⁸). Throughput: ~300 million additions/second per sphere.

### III.2 Pattern Classification Engine

**Task:** Classify input vectors into one of C classes (e.g., image recognition, signal classification).

**Configuration:** Array of C spheres, one per class, each tuned to a different periodic orbit. Input vector encoded as relative amplitudes across WGM modes of an input sphere. Classification by maximum coupling strength.

**Architecture:**

1. **Encoder sphere** (R = 100 μm, LiNbO₃ on insulator): Input data vector x = (x₁, ..., x_d) is encoded as amplitudes of d WGM modes using an array of d electro-optic modulators. Each modulator drives one mode to amplitude x_i.

2. **Class spheres** (R = 100 μm, fused silica, one per class): Each sphere is pre-programmed with a weight vector w_c = (w_{c,1}, ..., w_{c,d}) stored as mode amplitudes. The weight vector is a periodic orbit configuration — the billiard orbit that represents the learned template for class c.

3. **Coupling bus** (Si₃N₄ waveguides): The encoder sphere couples to all class spheres simultaneously via evanescent waveguides. The coupling transfers photons from encoder modes to class sphere modes.

4. **Detection:** The class sphere with the strongest output signal (most photons transferred) wins — it is the class whose stored template best matches the input. This is a winner-take-all classifier implemented entirely in photonic coupling strengths.

**ADE classification of the classifier:**

| Classes C | Minimum ADE type | Sphere arrangement | Symmetry |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|----------|
| 2 | A₁ | 2 spheres, linear | Z/2Z (binary) |
| 3 | A₂ | 3 spheres, triangle | Z/3Z (ternary) |
| 4 | D₄ | 4 spheres, star | Q₈ (quaternion) |
| 6 | D₆ or E₆ | 6 spheres, hexagonal or tetrahedral | Dih₆ or 2T |
| 24 | E₆ | 24-state, tetrahedral coupling | 2T (order 24) |
| 120 | E₈ | 120-state, icosahedral coupling | 2I (order 120) |

**For C > 120:** Stack multiple E₈ units. An array of N E₈ units provides 240N addressable states.

**Estimated performance:** Classification latency ~10 ns (limited by coupling transfer time). Throughput: ~100 million classifications/second. Power: ~1 mW total optical power (microwatt per sphere).

### III.3 Optimisation Processor (Simulated Annealing by Photon Thermalisation)

**Task:** Find the minimum of a cost function f(x) over a discrete domain.

**Configuration:** Multi-sphere array where the cost function is encoded in the coupling topology (inter-sphere gap distances and materials). The system's ground state — the lowest-energy WGM mode configuration — corresponds to the optimal solution.

**Mechanism:** Inject broadband (thermal) light into the array. The photons explore the mode space ergodically (irrational-angle orbits). Over time, the system thermalises: photons accumulate in the lowest-loss modes (highest Q, closest to periodic orbits). The steady-state mode distribution is a Boltzmann distribution over the billiard orbit energies. The dominant mode at equilibrium is the ground state = the optimal solution.

**Temperature control:** The effective temperature of the photon gas is controlled by the input light's spectral bandwidth. Broad spectrum = high temperature (wide exploration). Narrow spectrum = low temperature (exploitation of known good modes). Annealing schedule: start with broadband source, gradually narrow the bandwidth over ~1 μs.

**Encoding the cost function:** The cost f(x_i) for configuration x_i is mapped to the loss rate of the corresponding WGM mode. Modes with low cost have high Q (long lifetime, low loss); modes with high cost have low Q (short lifetime, high loss). The Q-factor of each mode is controlled by:

- Sphere surface roughness (lower Q for higher-cost modes — achievable by selective ion beam milling)
- Absorptive coatings (localized absorption at specific angular positions)
- Coupled scatterer particles (nanoparticles placed near the sphere surface to spoil specific modes)

**Estimated performance:** For a 240-state optimisation problem (one E₈ unit), thermalisation time ~100 ns. For larger problems, multiple E₈ units with inter-unit coupling; thermalisation scales as O(N log N) where N is the number of E₈ units.

### III.4 Fourier Transform Processor

**Task:** Compute the discrete Fourier transform of an N-point input signal.

**Configuration:** Single sphere (R = 100 μm) with N-mode input encoding.

**Mechanism:** The null billiard naturally performs a Fourier transform. The q-periodic orbit with K = (q, 0) is the result of summing q null momenta k_j = (1, cos(2πjp/q), sin(2πjp/q), 0). This sum is exactly the discrete Fourier transform evaluated at frequency p/q. By exciting a superposition of modes with amplitudes equal to the input signal, and allowing the billiard dynamics to evolve, the output mode spectrum IS the Fourier transform.

**Input:** Signal x = (x₀, x₁, ..., x_{N-1}) encoded as amplitudes of N consecutive WGM modes (l = l₀, l₀+1, ..., l₀+N-1).

**Output:** After one round-trip time, the mode spectrum has been mixed by the sphere's dispersion. The output mode amplitudes, read by spectral analysis, give X_k = Σ_j x_j exp(−2πijk/N) — the DFT.

**Performance:** Transform computed in one round-trip time (~3 ps for R = 100 μm). Throughput: ~300 billion FFT operations/second. This is 1000× faster than electronic FFT at comparable point count.

### III.5 Cryptographic Random Number Generator

**Task:** Generate true random bits from quantum vacuum fluctuations.

**Configuration:** Single sphere (R = 50 μm, Q ~ 10⁷) coupled to vacuum. No input laser.

**Mechanism:** Even without injected photons, the sphere's WGM modes are populated by quantum vacuum fluctuations (zero-point energy). These fluctuations are intrinsically random — they cannot be predicted even in principle. The OBSERVE operation (coupling to detector) collapses the vacuum state to a definite photon number, generating a random bit.

**Bit generation:** Each detection event (photon or no photon in a given time window) produces one random bit. At Q = 10⁷ with a 50 μm sphere, the mode lifetime is ~10 ns, giving a bit rate of ~100 Mbit/s per mode. Detecting across multiple modes simultaneously (using a spectrally resolved detector array) multiplies the rate by the number of resolved modes.

**Estimated performance:** 10 Gbit/s true random number generation from a single sphere with 100-mode parallel detection.

## IV. MULTI-SPHERE ARRANGEMENTS

### IV.1 Linear Chain (A_n topology)

**Geometry:** n spheres in a line, each coupled to its nearest neighbours. Coupling gaps ~500 nm (evanescent range).

**Topology:** A_{n-1} Dynkin diagram. Each sphere couples to at most 2 neighbours.

**Behaviour:** Photons propagate along the chain like phonons in a 1D crystal. Normal modes are standing waves with wavenumber k = mπ/(n+1), m = 1, ..., n. The chain acts as a delay line (signal propagation), a filter (only resonant modes transmit), or a shift register (photons hop sphere-to-sphere in discrete steps).

**Applications:** Signal delay (optical buffer), sequential logic, finite impulse response (FIR) filtering.

**Fabrication:** Align spheres in a V-groove etched in silicon. Gap controlled by sphere diameter and groove angle. Demonstrated in lab settings for up to 5 coupled spheres.

### IV.2 Ring (Cyclic A_n / Z_n topology)

**Geometry:** n spheres arranged in a circle, each coupled to both neighbours. The last sphere couples back to the first.

**Topology:** Cyclic group Z/nZ. This is the simplest topology with periodic boundary conditions.

**Behaviour:** Photons circulate around the ring. Normal modes are Bloch waves with wavenumber k = 2πm/n. The ring acts as a circulating memory (photons recirculate indefinitely in the low-loss limit), a ring oscillator (self-sustaining optical clock), or a cyclic permutation engine.

**Applications:** Optical memory buffer, clock generation, cyclic convolution, modular arithmetic (addition mod n).

### IV.3 Star (D_n topology)

**Geometry:** One central sphere coupled to n − 1 peripheral spheres. Peripheral spheres do not couple to each other.

**Topology:** D_n Dynkin diagram (for n ≥ 4). The central sphere is the branch node.

**Behaviour:** The central sphere acts as a router/mixer. Photons injected into any peripheral sphere are coupled to the centre, interact nonlinearly, and are redistributed to all peripherals. This implements a broadcast-and-combine operation.

**Applications:** Multi-operand addition (all peripheral values summed at centre), winner-take-all classification (strongest peripheral signal dominates), fan-out (one input distributed to many outputs).

### IV.4 Triangular Lattice (A₂ extended / hexagonal)

**Geometry:** Spheres arranged in a 2D triangular lattice. Each sphere couples to 6 nearest neighbours.

**Topology:** Extended A₂ lattice. The coordination number 6 matches the 6 roots of A₂.

**Behaviour:** Photons propagate in 2D with hexagonal symmetry. The lattice supports photonic band structure analogous to graphene's electronic bands — including Dirac cones at the K and K' points. This gives rise to topologically protected edge modes (photonic topological insulator behaviour).

**Applications:** 2D image processing (each sphere processes one pixel, coupled to 6 neighbours), topological routing (edge modes propagate without backscattering), large-scale parallel computation.

**Fabrication:** Self-assembly of microspheres on a patterned substrate. The substrate has a hexagonal array of dimples (etched by photolithography) that trap one sphere each. Demonstrated for close-packed silica sphere arrays up to ~10⁴ spheres.

### IV.5 E₈ Arrangement (Maximum Density)

**Geometry:** 8 spheres coupled with the E₈ Dynkin diagram topology: a chain of 7 spheres with one branch at the 5th node.

**Topology:** E₈. The coupling pattern follows the E₈ Dynkin diagram exactly.

**Behaviour:** The 240 roots of E₈ provide 240 distinct mode configurations (addressable states). Each state is a specific pattern of which modes are excited in which spheres. The topological protection of the ADE classification ensures that small perturbations cannot change the stored state — the system must undergo a discrete topological transition (analogous to a phase transition) to change from one root to another.

**Coupling specification:**
- Spheres 1–7 in a chain, gap = 500 nm (nearest-neighbour coupling J₁)
- Sphere 8 branches off sphere 5, gap = 500 nm (branch coupling J₂ = J₁)
- All other sphere pairs: gap > 5 μm (negligible coupling)
- Coupling strength J₁ ≈ 10 GHz for R = 50 μm silica spheres at 500 nm gap

**State addressing:** To write E₈ root index k (k = 0, ..., 239), inject photons into the 8 spheres with relative amplitudes and phases matching the components of root vector r_k. For example, root r₀ = (1, −1, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0) means: excite sphere 1 and sphere 2 with opposite phases, all other spheres dark.

**State readout:** Measure the mode amplitudes of all 8 spheres simultaneously. The measured 8-component vector is projected onto the nearest E₈ root — this is the stored state. The minimum distance between adjacent E₈ roots (√2 in normalised coordinates) provides the noise margin: any perturbation smaller than √2/2 ≈ 0.707 in any component leaves the stored root unchanged.

### IV.6 Hyperbolic Arrangement (Negative Curvature)

**Geometry:** Spheres arranged on a hyperbolic surface (physically approximated by a saddle-shaped substrate or a tree-like 3D scaffold).

**Topology:** Cayley tree or Bethe lattice with coordination number > 6.

**Behaviour:** Hyperbolic geometry provides exponentially more nodes within a given radius than Euclidean geometry. A Cayley tree of coordination 3 and depth d has 3 × 2^d − 2 nodes. This enables exponential memory scaling with linear depth.

**Applications:** Hierarchical classification (each level of the tree refines the classification), exponential-capacity memory, search algorithms (tree search performed by photon propagation).

**Fabrication challenge:** 3D sphere arrangement requires a scaffold structure. Candidate: 3D-printed polymer scaffold with spherical cavities at each node, into which microspheres are placed. Alternatively, use on-chip microring resonators connected in a tree topology by photolithographically defined waveguides.

## V. GATE IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS

### V.1 REFLECT Gate (Passive, Free)

Every total internal reflection at the sphere boundary is a REFLECT. No engineering required — it's intrinsic to WGM confinement. The REFLECT gate's fidelity is determined by the sphere's surface quality.

**Surface quality requirements:**

| Application | Surface roughness (RMS) | Achievable Q | Method |
|-------------|------------------------|--------------|--------|
| Phase 0 prototype | < 1 nm | ~10⁸ | CO₂ laser reflow |
| High-Q memory | < 0.1 nm | ~10⁹ | Flame polish + anneal |
| Ultra-high-Q (CaF₂) | < 0.05 nm | ~10¹¹ | Diamond turning + hand polish |

### V.2 CAPTURE Gate (Active, Nonlinear)

The CAPTURE gate requires two photons to interact inside the sphere. Implementation options:

**Option A: Kerr four-wave mixing (χ³)**
- Mechanism: Two pump photons at frequencies ω₁ and ω₂ generate signal and idler at ω₃ = 2ω₁ − ω₂ and ω₄ = 2ω₂ − ω₁.
- Material: Fused silica (weak but broadband) or Si₃N₄ (10× stronger).
- Threshold power: ~10 μW in a silica sphere with Q = 10⁸ and R = 50 μm.
- Speed: Instantaneous (limited only by photon round-trip time, ~ps).
- Fidelity: High for frequency-matched modes; parasitic mixing produces unwanted sidebands that must be filtered.

**Option B: Second-harmonic generation (χ²)**
- Mechanism: Two photons at ω combine to one photon at 2ω.
- Material: LiNbO₃ (strong χ² response, phase-matchable).
- Threshold power: ~1 μW.
- Speed: Instantaneous.
- Advantage: Cleaner spectrum (fewer parasitic products). Disadvantage: Requires phase matching, limiting the range of usable mode pairs.

**Option C: Stimulated Brillouin scattering**
- Mechanism: Photon interacts with acoustic phonon, producing a frequency-shifted photon. Effectively a photon-phonon-photon interaction.
- Material: Any glass sphere (universal in dielectrics).
- Threshold power: ~100 μW for R = 100 μm silica sphere.
- Speed: Limited by acoustic transit time (~ns).
- Advantage: Very narrow linewidth output (< 1 Hz), useful for precision applications.

**Option D: Single-photon transistor (Purdue/Sychev 2025)**
- Mechanism: One photon triggers avalanche multiplication in a doped silicon region, producing a controlled multi-photon output.
- Material: CMOS-compatible silicon, integrated on-chip.
- Speed: GHz (demonstrated).
- Advantage: True single-photon nonlinearity. Disadvantage: Requires silicon integration, not compatible with free-standing microspheres in early prototypes.

### V.3 OBSERVE Gate (Readout)

**Spectral readout options:**

| Method | Resolution | Speed | Complexity | Best for |
|--------|-----------|-------|-----------|---------|
| Scanning Fabry-Pérot | ~1 MHz | ~ms | Low | Phase 0 lab characterisation |
| Optical frequency comb | ~100 Hz | ~μs | High | Precision mode identification |
| Arrayed waveguide grating | ~10 GHz | ~ns | Medium | On-chip integration (Phase 3+) |
| Heterodyne with local oscillator | ~1 Hz | ~μs | Medium | Phase-resolved readout |
| SPAD array + spectral filter | ~1 nm | ~ns | Low | Fast classification readout |

## VI. THERMAL AND ENVIRONMENTAL MANAGEMENT

### VI.1 Thermal Stability

WGM resonance frequencies shift with temperature at rate dν/dT ≈ −(1/n)(dn/dT)ν. For silica at 1550 nm, this is ~1.5 GHz/K. A single FSR at R = 50 μm is ~650 GHz. So the temperature must be stable to within ~430 K to keep modes within one FSR — trivially satisfied at room temperature.

However, for high-q orbits where adjacent modes must be individually resolved, the requirement tightens. Resolving modes separated by FSR/q requires thermal stability of ~430/q Kelvin. For q = 100, this is ±4.3 K (easy). For q = 1000, it's ±0.43 K (modest thermoelectric cooling). For q = 10000, it's ±0.043 K (precision temperature control, commercially available).

**Recommendation:** For Phase 0–2, standard laboratory temperature control (±0.1 K) suffices for orbits up to q ~ 4000. For Phase 3+ with high-q E₈ states, use a thermoelectric cooler with PID feedback (±0.01 K), commercially available for ~$500.

### VI.2 Vibration Isolation

Mechanical vibration modulates the sphere-fibre coupling gap, causing amplitude noise on the OBSERVE signal. The critical frequency range is 10 Hz – 10 kHz (building vibrations, acoustic noise). The coupling gap must be stable to within ~1 nm to maintain constant coupling strength.

**Mitigation:** Mount the sphere-fibre assembly on a vibration-isolated optical breadboard (commercial pneumatic isolators provide > 20 dB isolation above 10 Hz). For Phase 3+ on-chip integration, vibration is negligible because the spheres and waveguides are monolithically fabricated on a rigid substrate.

### VI.3 Humidity and Contamination

Silica microspheres degrade in humid air: water molecules adsorb to the surface, increasing scattering loss and reducing Q. CaF₂ is even more sensitive to moisture.

**Mitigation:** Operate in dry nitrogen atmosphere (glove box or sealed package with desiccant). For on-chip integration (Phase 3+), hermetic packaging with nitrogen fill — standard in photonics packaging.

## VIII. NEAR-TERM EXPERIMENTAL PRIORITIES

### Experiment 0A: Mode Spectrum Mapping (Month 1–3)
Fabricate a single silica sphere (R = 50 μm). Couple with tapered fibre. Sweep tuneable laser across 1540–1560 nm. Record transmission spectrum. Identify WGM mode families (l, m, n_r). Verify FSR matches theory. Deliverable: validated mode map for a single sphere.

### Experiment 0B: Orbit Identification (Month 3–6)
Using the mapped sphere, selectively excite individual WGM modes by tuning the laser to specific resonances. For each excited mode, measure the spatial field distribution using near-field scanning optical microscopy (NSOM) or far-field scattering pattern. Verify that mode l = 3 shows a triangular scattering pattern, l = 4 shows square, l = 5 shows pentagonal. Deliverable: visual confirmation that WGM modes correspond to null billiard periodic orbits.

### Experiment 1A: Nonlinear Mode Mixing (Month 6–12)
Inject two laser beams at frequencies ω₁ and ω₂ (corresponding to modes l₁ = 3 and l₂ = 5) into the same sphere. Monitor the output spectrum for four-wave mixing products at ω₃ = 2ω₁ − ω₂ and ω₄ = 2ω₂ − ω₁. If the product at ω₃ corresponds to mode l₃ = 1 (= 2×3 − 5) and ω₄ to l₄ = 7 (= 2×5 − 3), this confirms the CAPTURE gate performs arithmetic on orbit numbers. Deliverable: demonstrated photonic addition/subtraction of billiard orbit numbers.

### Experiment 1B: Addition Demonstration (Month 9–12)
Perform the 3 + 5 = 8 computation described in the original prototype spec. Inject modes l = 3 and l = 5. Detect output at l = 8 via stimulated four-wave mixing (ω₃ = ω₁ + ω₂ in the sum-frequency case, requiring a χ² material or cascaded χ³ process). Deliverable: first null-geometric photonic arithmetic operation.

### Experiment 2: Two-Sphere Coupling (Month 12–24)
Bring two spheres within evanescent range. Characterise coupling strength vs. gap distance. Demonstrate photon tunnelling between spheres. Measure the 5 observable parameters of the 2-NULBIT register. Deliverable: first multi-sphere null register.
